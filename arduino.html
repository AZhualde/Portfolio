ARDUINO PROJECT

BIKE SENSE

project aims to create a real-time embedded safety system tailored for bicycle riders, focusing on detecting and warning about objects in their blind spots. Given the substantial risk blind-spot collisions pose to riders, the system aims to enhance safety by alerting them to potential hazards. It will utilize sensors capable of detecting speed, distance, and objects within the bicycle's blind spot. Data from these sensors will be processed by an application, triggering alerts through display, lights, and speaker outputs in real-time. A fusion algorithm will merge sensor data to improve object detection accuracy and minimize false positives. Operating on an embedded platform, the system will process sensor data instantaneously, presenting visual and audible alerts through a graphical user interface.

Arduino

Arduino is an open-source electronics platform based on easy-to-use hardware and software. Arduino boards can read inputs - a light on a sensor, a finger on a button, or a Twitter message - and turn them into an output - activating a motor, turning on an LED, or publishing something online. You can tell your board what to do by sending a set of instructions to the microcontroller on the board. To do so you use the Arduino programming language (based on Wiring), and the Arduino Software (IDE), based on Processing. Arduino was born at the Ivrea Interaction Design Institute as an easy tool for fast prototyping, aimed at students without a background in electronics and programming. As soon as it reached a wider community, the Arduino board started changing to adapt to new needs and challenges, differentiating its offer from simple 8-bit boards to products for IoT applications, wearables, 3D printing, and embedded environments.

BLUETOOTH / SENSOR WIRING

wiring of Bluetooth and sensors to an Arduino board depends on the specific Bluetooth module and sensors you are using. However, I can provide a general overview of how you might wire these components.

Bluetooth Module:

Most commonly, Bluetooth modules like the HC-05 or HC-06 are used with Arduino. These modules typically have four pins: VCC, GND, TX, and RX. You would connect the VCC pin to a 5V pin on the Arduino for power and the GND pin to a ground pin. The TX pin on the Bluetooth module should be connected to the RX pin (receive) on the Arduino, and the RX pin on the Bluetooth module should be connected to the TX pin (transmit) on the Arduino. This setup allows the Arduino to communicate with the Bluetooth module.

Sensor(s):

Sensors come in various types and often have different wiring configurations. However, most sensors will have at least three pins: VCC, GND, and an analog or digital signal pin. You would connect the VCC pin to a 5V pin on the Arduino for power and the GND pin to a ground pin. The signal pin would then be connected to a digital or analog pin on the Arduino, depending on the type of sensor and how you want to read data from it.

ESP32 WIRING

wire an ESP32-CAM (ESP32 Camera Module) to an Arduino board, you'll typically need to use jumper wires to connect the necessary pins. Here's a basic overview of how to wire the ESP32-CAM to an Arduino.

ESP32-CAM Pinout:

The ESP32-CAM module typically has several pins, including power pins, GPIO pins, and camera-related pins. The specific pinout may vary slightly depending on the manufacturer, but the main pins you'll need to connect are typically as follows.

Arduino Pins:

You'll need to identify the corresponding pins on your Arduino board for connecting to the ESP32-CAM. Typically, you'll use digital pins for communication with the ESP32-CAM's UART interface (for serial communication) and other pins for power and ground connections.

Wiring:

VCC (ESP32-CAM) -> 3.3V (Arduino)

GND (ESP32-CAM) -> GND (Arduino)

U0T (ESP32-CAM) -> RX (Arduino)

You'll use this pin for transmitting data from the ESP32-CAM to the Arduino.

U0R (ESP32-CAM) -> TX (Arduino)

You'll use this pin for receiving data from the Arduino to the ESP32-CAM.

IO0 (ESP32-CAM) -> GND (Arduino)

This pin is typically connected to ground to enable normal operation mode.

EN (ESP32-CAM) -> 3.3V (Arduino)

This pin is typically connected to 3.3V to enable the ESP32-CAM module.

IO5, IO14 (ESP32-CAM)

These pins are used for the camera interface and may need to be connected to the corresponding pins on the ESP32-CAM board.

Considerations:

Ensure that the voltage levels are compatible between the ESP32-CAM and the Arduino

(3.3V for ESP32-CAM and typically 5V for Arduino).

You may need to use a level shifter or voltage divider to ensure that the TX pin of the Arduino

(which operates at 5V) does not damage the RX pin of the ESP32-CAM (which operates at 3.3V).

It's essential to check the datasheets and documentation

specifically ESP32-CAM module and Arduino board for any additional wiring considerations or compatibility issues.
